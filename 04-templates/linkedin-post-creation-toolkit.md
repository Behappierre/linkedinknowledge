# LinkedIn Post Creation Toolkit
## Quick Reference for Writing High-Performing Content

---

## Pre-Writing Checklist

**Before you start drafting, answer these questions:**

- [ ] Do I have a borrowed credibility quote? (If no, get one first)
- [ ] Is this quote specific and operational? (Not generic consultant-speak)
- [ ] Do I have permission to use it? (Or can I anonymize?)
- [ ] Do I have concrete numbers/examples? (At least 2-3 specific details)
- [ ] Do I have a visual? (Photo, chart, or relevant image)
- [ ] What's my orchestration insight? (How does coordination solve this?)
- [ ] What's my engagement question? (How will I end?)
- [ ] Is this Tuesday-Thursday 7-9am GMT posting time? (Optimal timing)

**If you can't answer yes to most of these, pause and gather more material first.**

## The 7-Element Structure (Every Successful Post Has These)

### Element 1: Borrowed Credibility Opening (Lines 1-2)
**Start with operator's authentic voice, not your own**

**Good examples:**
- "A missed charge at 2pm becomes a service failure at 6pm."
- "Tuesdays and Thursdays." That was the answer when I asked...
- "Learning to dance together is challenging but rewarding."
- "The sensors detected movement 4 hours before the Shap landslip. But it was below the 10mm threshold."

**Bad examples (avoid):**
- "Proud to have represented Netcompany at..."
- "I recently attended a fascinating conference..."
- "Investing in X can enhance Y. However..."
- "Digital transformation is challenging in today's environment..."

**Formula:** 
[Powerful quote] + [Who said it / what situation]

---

### Element 2: The "I Learned" Frame (Lines 3-5)
**Show you listen and learn, don't just know**

**Good examples:**
- "I'd assumed a diesel mindset: charge overnight, run all day. Reality is opportunity charging..."
- "When I asked what determines their fleet size, I expected 'passenger demand.' Instead..."
- "I thought the problem was technology. Turns out..."

**Why this works:**
- Shows humility
- Makes you relatable
- Demonstrates you update thinking
- Signals you listen to operators

**Formula:**
"I assumed [X], but learned [Y]" or "I expected [X], instead [Y]"

---

### Element 3: Concrete Operational Details (Lines 6-10)
**Prove you understand the operation with specific examples**

**What to include:**
- Specific numbers ("30-35% of time", "48 hours out of 8,712")
- System names ("pantograph chargers", "RAIB advisory")
- Operational scenarios ("wrong bay, bad connection, late arrival")
- Timing details ("2pm charge failure → 6pm service failure")
- Named routes/locations ("Great Eastern Main Line", "Liverpool Street-Shenfield")

**Pattern to follow:**
1. State the challenge
2. Give specific example
3. Explain why current approach fails
4. Show cascading impact

**Formula:**
[Problem] + [Specific example] + [Why it matters] + [What happens when it fails]

---

### Element 4: The Orchestration Insight (Lines 11-14)
**Connect to your core theme: coordination over technology**

**Key phrases that work:**
- "This isn't a technology problem, it's a coordination challenge"
- "Not smarter algorithms, smarter coordination"
- "The challenge is synchronization between [system A] + [system B] + [system C]"
- "When systems don't talk to each other..."
- "Making complexity invisible so growth can appear"

**What to emphasize:**
- Human + system coordination (not just technical)
- Multiple systems need to work together
- Timing and sequencing matter
- Current approaches fail at coordination layer

**Formula:**
"The real challenge isn't [technology] - it's [coordination between X, Y, Z]"

---

### Element 5: Proof or Validation (Lines 15-17)
**Show this isn't just your opinion**

**Types of proof:**
- Operator response ("Dominic Todd explained the 'set swap' issue")
- Industry data ("£355 million annually", "25+ border agencies")
- Practitioner validation ("Network Rail engineer said: 'This is the first time...'")
- Real system output ("LARA was producing: 'RED risk on GEML...'")
- Multiple sources agreeing ("Across conversations with leaders from Airbus, Caterpillar, Mars...")

**Formula:**
[Authority figure] + [specific quote or data] + [what it proves]

---

### Element 6: Credit and Attribution (Lines 18-19)
**Name the people who helped, credit your sources**

**Who to credit:**
- Quoted operators (by name, title, company)
- Conference speakers
- Team members (if hackathon/project)
- Organizations (Network Rail, The Chartered Institute, etc.)

**How to do it:**
- @ tag them in body text where mentioned
- Add line at end if multiple people: "Thanks to [names]"
- For anonymous: "An operator I spoke with" or "A manufacturing leader"

**Why this matters:**
- Social proof
- Signals you're part of community
- Increases chance they'll engage
- Builds relationships

**Formula:**
Throughout post: [Name, Title @ LinkedIn] shared that...
At end: "Great to attend alongside [names]" or "Thanks to [team members]"

---

### Element 7: Engagement Question (Final Line)
**End with invitation, not conclusion**

**Good engagement questions:**
- "Does any other sector have such an extreme Peak Load ratio?"
- "For operators scaling beyond pilots: what breaks first, the operating model, the grid constraint, or the technology stack?"
- "What stops AI adoption in your industry—technology or trust?"
- "How quickly do your monitoring investments become operationally useful?"
- "If we can build this in 8 hours, why doesn't it exist already?"

**Bad endings (avoid):**
- "Thank you to my team." (closes conversation)
- "Looking forward to next steps." (vague, no invitation)
- "Great to attend alongside..." (just stops)
- Any statement that doesn't invite response

**Formula:**
- Compare to other sectors ("Does [your sector] experience [similar challenge]?")
- Challenge assumptions ("Why doesn't [obvious solution] exist already?")
- Invite experience ("What breaks first when you scale [process]?")
- Stage-aware question ("For those moving beyond pilots...")

**When stuck on closing question, use one of these patterns:**

### 1. Cross-Sector Comparison

## Visual Selection Guide

### When to Use Photos (BEST for viral reach)
**Situations:**
- Real-time observations (train display, wrong information)
- Event attendance (conference, panel, team)
- Physical evidence of problem
- Human element needed

**Impact:** Stops scrolling, feels authentic, universal appeal

**Requirements:**
- High quality (not blurry)
- Clear subject
- Visible context
- Readable on mobile

---

### When to Use Charts/Visualizations (BEST for precision targeting)
**Situations:**
- Technical insights
- Complex concepts need simplification
- Comparing scenarios
- Demonstrating scale/scope

**Impact:** Professional credibility, analytical thinking

**Requirements:**
- One key insight per chart
- Clear labels
- Strategic color use
- Mobile-readable text

**Tools:** PowerPoint, Canva, Excel (simple is better)

---

### When to Use Metaphor Images (GOOD for thought leadership)
**Situations:**
- Abstract concepts need grounding
- Want memorable imagery
- Conference themes

**Impact:** Memorable, shareable, creative

**Examples:**
- Ballroom dancers (rail coordination)
- Rubik's cube (innovation sequencing)
- Robots on surface, pipes below (data infrastructure)

## Hashtag Selection Formula

**Always 4 hashtags maximum (never more than 5)**

**Structure:**
- 1 industry-specific (#RailTransport, #ElectricBus, #Manufacturing)
- 1-2 topic-specific (#FleetManagement, #AssetOptimization, #GridConstraint)
- 1 your theme (#Orchestration, #SystemsOrchestration)
- 0-1 geographic if relevant (#UKRail, #RailEurope)

**Good examples:**
- #ElectricBus #FleetManagement #ZeroEmission #Orchestration
- #RailTransport #FleetManagement #AssetOptimization #Orchestration
- #RailSafety #NetworkRail #SystemsOrchestration #RailInfrastructure

**Avoid:**
- Generic (#Innovation, #DigitalTransformation, #Leadership, #Technology)
- Too many (6+ looks spammy)
- Unrelated (#AI when post isn't about AI)

## Red Flags: Stop and Rewrite If You See These

**Opening line red flags:**
- ❌ "Proud to have represented..."
- ❌ "Delighted to announce..."
- ❌ "I recently attended..."
- ❌ "Fascinating discussion about..."
- ❌ "Honored to be invited..."

**Content red flags:**
- ❌ No specific numbers (all abstract language)
- ❌ No named people (all generic "stakeholders")
- ❌ Bullet points (looks like PowerPoint)
- ❌ Buzzwords without explanation ("synergies", "transformation")
- ❌ No engagement question (just stops)

**Structural red flags:**
- ❌ More than 5 hashtags
- ❌ No visual included
- ❌ Longer than 300 words
- ❌ Posted Friday afternoon or weekend
- ❌ No @ tags of relevant people

**Tone red flags:**
- ❌ Sounds like press release
- ❌ Selling/promotional language
- ❌ Overly formal/academic
- ❌ Jargon-heavy without explanation
- ❌ No human voice (sounds like AI wrote it)

**If you see 3+ red flags, stop and rewrite before posting.**

## The 30-Minute Draft Process

**Minute 0-5: Gather Materials**
- Pull operator quote or observation
- Find/create visual
- List 2-3 specific numbers/examples
- Identify orchestration angle

**Minute 5-15: Draft Structure**
- Write opening (borrowed credibility line)
- Add "I learned" frame
- Insert concrete details
- State orchestration insight
- Add proof/validation
- Credit sources
- Write engagement question

**Minute 15-25: Refine**
- Remove bullet points
- Check no red flags
- Verify @ tags correct
- Ensure 4 hashtags
- Mobile readability check

**Minute 25-30: Final Review**
- Run through pre-post quality check
- Read aloud (does it sound natural?)
- Empathy check (would quoted person approve?)
- Schedule or post

**If can't finish in 30 minutes, you don't have enough material yet. Go gather more before drafting.**

## Emergency Scenarios: What to Do When...

### When You Don't Have Operator Quote
**Options:**
1. Postpone post until you get quote (best option)
2. Use conference/webinar quote from public source
3. Use anonymous attribution ("An operator I spoke with...")
4. Use your own real-time observation instead
5. Frame as question inviting operator insights

**Don't:** Make up quotes, use vague "stakeholders said", proceed without any authentic voice

---

### When Post Isn't Getting Engagement
**After 30 minutes, no reactions:**
- Add self-comment with additional context
- DM the people you @ tagged
- Share in relevant LinkedIn groups (sparingly)
- Don't panic - some posts build slowly

**After 6 hours, <200 impressions:**
- Check: wrong posting time? (Friday afternoon?)
- Check: no visual included?
- Check: abstract opening not compelling?
- Document what didn't work
- Move on to next post

**After 24 hours, underperforming:**
- Still may build over 48 hours (precision content)
- Extract learnings for future posts
- Don't delete (shows inconsistency)
- Focus energy on next post

---

### When Quoted Person Doesn't Engage
**They liked but didn't comment:**
- This is normal (Lars example)
- Content should work standalone
- Thank them privately via DM
- Don't take personally

**They didn't react at all:**
- May not have seen it (LinkedIn algorithm)
- May not be active on LinkedIn
- May need time to respond
- Follow up privately if important

**They react negatively:**
- Apologize immediately
- Offer to remove/edit
- Understand their concern
- Maintain relationship over content
- Learn for next time

---

### When Post Goes Unexpectedly Viral
**Impressions suddenly spike:**
- Monitor comments closely (respond quickly)
- Watch for any controversial thread developing
- Engage deeply with quality comments
- Track demographics (is right audience?)
- Document what made it work

**Getting negative attention:**
- Stay professional in responses
- Don't argue or defend excessively
- Acknowledge different perspectives
- Consider if need to clarify anything
- May need to mute notifications if overwhelming

**Opportunities emerging:**
- Business inquiries → respond promptly
- Speaking invitations → discuss with Netcompany
- Media requests → loop in communications team
- Collaboration proposals → evaluate strategically
