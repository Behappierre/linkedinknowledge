"We already have more zero-emission buses than any other city in the UK has buses."

Andy Lord, Commissioner at Transport for London, said that last night at the London Transport Museum corporate members reception marking the launch of the LTM50 capital campaign for the museum's 50th anniversary in 2030.

The number behind that statement was 3,000: the scale of London's electric bus fleet now coming into view.

I had been thinking and wrote about the charging orchestration challenge at 100 buses, maybe 200. I had not fully reckoned with what it looks like at 3,000 vehicles, operated by multiple companies across dozens of depots, all running to a single network specification.

At that scale, the coordination problem does not sit inside one organisation. It spans them.

Shared grid constraints. Overnight charging windows. Bay allocation. Maintenance schedules. Different operators. Different systems. Same network outcome required by morning.

These are no longer edge cases. They are the operating model.

Andy Lord also noted that TfL now has funding certainty to 2030 for the first time in more than seven years. That matters not just for vehicle procurement, but for the coordination infrastructure a fleet this size demands.

The vehicles were always going to arrive. The question is whether the orchestration layer arrives with them.

For operators running electric fleets at scale, which coordination challenge grows fastest as vehicle numbers rise?
