## Writing Rules (ABSOLUTE)

### Never Use Em Dashes (—)
**Rule:** Do not use the em dash character (—) anywhere in LinkedIn posts. Ever.

**Why:** Em dashes are a typographic convention that can feel formal or editorial. They interrupt conversational flow and are a subtle signal of AI-generated or over-edited text.

**Alternatives:**
- Use a comma instead: "politicians shouldn't run railways, experts should"
- Use a colon instead: "one goal: better passenger experience"
- Use a full stop and new sentence: "This layer is moving. The question is what comes next."
- Use a hyphen for compound adjectives: "track-train governance"
- Rephrase to avoid the pause entirely

**This rule applies to all post drafts, templates, and examples going forward.**

## Critical Lessons (Cumulative)

### Never Qualify Borrowed Credibility (ABSOLUTE RULE - confirmed repeatedly)

Groundhog Day (1,088 impressions): "Not the exact word but I paraphrase" killed authenticity.
George Bradshaw (11,468 impressions): Clean ministerial quote, no qualification, 60 reactions.
EU Telematics (3,255 impressions): Clean CER quote, no qualification.

**Rule:** Direct quotes only. Never qualify. If you cannot quote directly, anonymize completely without announcing reconstruction. If the quote's original context differs from your post's context, bridge it explicitly - "He wasn't just talking about X. He was talking about Y" - this builds credibility rather than hiding the shift.

---

### Compliance Framing Weakens Policy Arguments (NEW - EU Telematics)

**Wrong:** "GBR must align with EU standards" (factually incorrect and weaker)
**Right:** "Great Britain is outside the EU's legal framework. But not outside its commercial consequences."

The second version is more accurate, more interesting, and harder to dismiss. It positions the UK as making a strategic choice, not failing a compliance test.

**Rule:** For UK-EU policy posts, always frame as strategic choice not compliance obligation.

---

### Transparent Context Extension Builds Credibility (NEW - EU Telematics)

When using a quote in a slightly different context than its original, acknowledge the extension explicitly:
"He wasn't just talking about tracks and signals. He was talking about the digital plumbing underneath them."

This is stronger than hiding the contextual shift. Technical readers who know the original source will respect the honesty.

---

### The Fragmentation Reveal (NEW - EU Telematics)

The most shareable line in the EU Telematics post:
"So the UK doesn't have one digital architecture question to answer. It has several, and they're not yet being asked in the same room."

This type of structural insight - revealing that a problem is more fragmented across owners than it appears - is highly shareable and requires genuine sector knowledge to produce.

**Rule:** Look for the fragmentation reveal in any governance or coordination topic. Who owns which part? Are they talking to each other? This framing consistently generates engagement from senior decision-makers.

---

### Technical Specialist Audience Lowers Seniority % Acceptably (NEW - EU Telematics)

Posts about technical standards and data architecture attract engineers (Entry level) alongside executives. EU Telematics had 20.3% Entry, pulling seniority to 58%.

**Rule:** For technical standards posts, accept 55-60% seniority as the target rather than 60-66%. The entry-level audience are the engineers making the decisions the post is about.

---

### Two Posts One Day - First Post Wins (NEW - EU Telematics / Heathrow)

When forced to post twice on the same day, the first post dominates engagement. The second receives less algorithmic attention.

**Rule:** If posting twice on one day is unavoidable, ensure the strategically more important post goes first. Never post twice by choice.

---

### Minister-Level Borrowed Credibility Unlocks Policy Audience (George Bradshaw)

**Rule:** When content involves government or policy, open with the most senior government voice available. Minister > Infrastructure Chief > Operator is the credibility hierarchy.

---

### Named Frameworks Generate Richer Comments (George Bradshaw)

**Rule:** Named frameworks invite extension and debate. Give people a structure to agree with, disagree with, or build on. Comments become richer and longer than simple observation posts.

---

### Multi-Source Borrowed Credibility Creates Layered Authority (George Bradshaw)

Stack borrowed credibility from multiple authority levels for policy posts. Reader cannot dismiss by questioning one source.

---

### Scotland Geography Pre-Seeding Works (George Bradshaw)

Mentioning an upcoming speaking engagement in a post seeds that geography in the audience. Glasgow reached 3.5% when Scotland panel was mentioned.

**Rule:** When you have an upcoming speaking engagement, mention it in the post.

---

### They Will Use Your Language (Railway Within Railway)

Steve White wrote "whole systems thinking and collaboration" - orchestration positioning in his words. You are shaping how industry leaders describe the problem.

**Rule:** Keep using "systems thinking," "coordination," "orchestration." It is becoming the vocabulary of the debate.

---

### Soft Tech Positioning Protects and Strengthens Relationships (Railway Within Railway)

When the quoted source is a potential client, frame platform need as open question rather than implied gap:
"The question is whether the coordination layer can deliver on the ambition."

Result: Source approves the post by reacting, and others comment substantively.
