"We've missed our slot. The next available slot is in two and a half hours."

My pilot's announcement meant we sat on the tarmac for two hours. At the time, I didn't understand the mechanics.

Then I visited Copenhagen Airport and saw Best Planned, First Served in action.

The rule is absolute: Keep to your plan, keep your priority. Miss your slot? You go to the back of the queue.

As an Operations Director told me: "A captain panics if he's going to miss his slot. He knows he'll be back in the line."

Because the pain is immediate and visible (crew duty, connections, rotations), everyone, airlines, handlers, airport, all share the data.

This is the lesson for GBR and rail reform.

We already prioritise on-time trains. But the decision is often manual, opaque, and argued about later in delay attribution.

The difference in Copenhagen isn't the principle; it's the execution. The consequences are automated, visible, and accepted by everyone in real-time.
That's the gap between Control and Orchestration.

Organisational alignment without the technology to make it visible is just a meeting. They have to arrive together.

What would "Best Planned, First Served" look like for rail?

## 12. Lessons Learned

### Aviation-Primary Content Achieves 10%+ Airlines and Aviation Sector Reach

This post and subsequent aviation posts (Heathrow, AIRHART) confirmed the pattern: aviation sector concentration scales with aviation's prominence in the content. When aviation is the primary subject, Airlines and Aviation reaches 7-10%. When aviation is supporting evidence, it falls to 3-5% or disappears.

**Rule:** Use aviation as a primary subject when targeting aviation operators and airport professionals. Do not use aviation as supporting evidence for rail posts if the goal is to open the aviation channel.

---

Three European capital geographies each above 2% in a single post. This is the strongest European signal in the series (other than Brussels in the EU Telematics post).
