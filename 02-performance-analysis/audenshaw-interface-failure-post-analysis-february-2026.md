**Post text (verbatim):**

In rail safety, two teams can both do their job, and the risk can still fall through the interface.

In December 2020, Network Rail's structures team identified serious defects at a bridge in Audenshaw: broken screws, missing clips, displaced packing pieces. The findings were recorded in structures inspection reports.

In August 2023, the track maintenance team inspected the same location. They again found broken screws and voiding. The defects were repaired locally, but the systemic warning wasn't flagged.

On 6 September 2024, a freight train derailed at that exact spot.

How did we miss it?

The RAIB investigation found "no evidence of any joint visits" between the track and structures disciplines.

Each team saw part of the problem.
- Structures saw the bridge.
- Track saw the rail.
- The warnings never combined into a single, shared safety picture.

The RAIB's conclusion is blunt: Network Rail must "improve the interface between the two disciplines."

This isn't just a maintenance error. It's an orchestration failure.

When safety-critical signals live in different systems - structures inspection reports in one database, track defects in Ellipse - they don't compound into a safety case. They fragment into "someone else's responsibility".

For those working on the Great British Railways transition: where do your critical warnings live? Are your teams seeing the whole picture, or just their half?

**Content violations confirmed on review:**

1. **Bullet points in body:** Three-item list (Structures saw the bridge / Track saw the rail / The warnings never combined). This is the most visible structural error. Bullet points carry a confirmed algorithmic penalty and visually signal PowerPoint-style content.

2. **Five hashtags:** #RailSafety #NetworkRail #SystemsThinking #Orchestration #Audenshaw — maximum is four. #Audenshaw is geographically specific but adds no audience targeting value.

3. **No borrowed credibility opening:** Post opens with a general observation ("In rail safety, two teams can both do their job..."). This is the writer's framing, not an operator or practitioner voice. Compare to Helpston: "183 times." — the RAIB data point as opening number.

4. **No named framework:** The post correctly identifies the failure mode (discipline interface gap, signals fragmenting into "someone else's responsibility") but does not name it. The Audenshaw failure is a legitimate, nameable pattern — something like "Interface Blindspot" or "Discipline Fragmentation" — that could have been defined and made transferable. Without it, the post is descriptive rather than conceptual.

## Lessons Learned

**Confirmed: Named framework is the mechanism for RAIB post virality.**
The Audenshaw failure mode is a genuinely transferable organisational pattern. It has a name waiting to be coined — "Interface Blindspot", "Discipline Silo Failure", "Split Custody Gap", or similar. Without it, the post tops out at Good tier regardless of the underlying quality. This lesson was already documented from Helpston vs Shap; Audenshaw provides a third data point confirming the rule.

**Bullet points confirmed as ceiling limiter at 1,539 impressions.**
The content was structurally sound aside from the list. The three-item bullet list is the most visible violation and the easiest to fix in future drafts.

**Five hashtags confirmed as a deduction.**
#Audenshaw added no audience value and nudged the post into the penalised range. Geographically specific incident hashtags are not useful targeting tools on LinkedIn.

**No named framework = no comments = no comment thread quality.**
Helpston generated 22 comments including four sectors and two RAIB precedents. Audenshaw generated 0. The difference is not subject matter or audience — it is the presence of a concept to react to.
