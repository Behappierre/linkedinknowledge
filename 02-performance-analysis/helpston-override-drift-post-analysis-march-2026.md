"183 times."

That's how many times the sealed release plunger was used at Helpston signal box in the eight months before a car driver and pedestrians found themselves at a level crossing with a freight train approaching.

The interlocking had done its job. When the signaller tried to manually raise the barriers, the system detected the DCRail Class 60 approaching on the Up Stamford line and blocked the raise. The safeguard worked exactly as designed.

The signaller, having recently experienced a barrier fault at the same crossing, assumed the system had failed again. He broke the seal and pressed the plunger, bypassing the interlocking entirely.

Fourteen seconds later he saw the train.

The RAIB Safety Digest describes what followed as a local practice that had "developed" at Helpston, undocumented in local instructions and unknown to managers. An emergency override, intended for specific Rule Book circumstances, had quietly become routine during possessions.

This is what I would call Override Drift: the observed pattern where an exceptional measure, used repeatedly without detection, migrates from the edge of the rulebook into the centre of daily practice. The override no longer signals an abnormal condition. It signals normal operations. And your assurance layer cannot tell the difference.

The broken seal went unrepaired for four months. It had been logged. It had not been escalated. The system recorded 183 uses. The data was there. The visibility was not.

The technology that should have prevented this worked. The assurance and oversight around how it was being used did not.

How does your organisation detect when an emergency procedure has quietly become the standard one?

## 12. Lessons Learned

### RAIB Named Framework Pattern Confirmed for Third Time

Override Drift is the third named framework from a RAIB report, following Shap Landslip ("Correlation Gap", "Deployment Gap"). All three achieved viral or near-viral reach through slow-burn distribution, high save rates, and cross-industry comment thread validation.

**Rule:** RAIB reports are a reliable and recurring source of named framework material. Read the report, identify the systemic failure, name the mechanism as an observable organisational pattern, define it clinically, connect to the assurance or coordination layer. The framework must be transferable beyond rail.

---

### Named Frameworks Can Enter Professional Practice Discourse Within 24 Hours

Allan Spence cited Override Drift at the Global Level Crossing Network meeting the day after the post was published. This is the completion of the thought leadership cycle: post → professional validation in comments → cited in professional practice.

**Rule:** A named framework that is genuinely useful will be picked up by practitioners within 24-48 hours and used in their own professional contexts. You do not control this — you create the conditions for it. The clinical definition, the transferability, and the RAIB source credibility all contributed.

---

### "The Technology Worked" Inversion Protects Target Account Relationships

Daniel Fisk's connection request confirms this framing. NR professionals engaged rather than going quiet.

**Rule:** Lead with what worked before naming what failed. Criticism becomes analysis.

---

### Practitioner Corrections Deepen Frameworks Rather Than Undermine Them

Daniel Pyke's correction (the seal was broken 4 months before the incident, not during it) sharpened Override Drift: the physical evidence of the drift had itself become invisible. Richard Howells' operational context (down 48/60 minutes, immense pressure, diverted bridge funding) explained the conditions that made drift almost inevitable. Both corrections strengthened the argument.

**Rule:** When practitioners correct factual details in comments, engage with the correction honestly and immediately. Then show how the corrected detail sharpens rather than undermines the systemic argument. This is the "concede and redirect" move — it demonstrates intellectual honesty and usually generates a richer reply.

Thomas Wood identified Craven Arms 2024 (RAIB Safety Digest 08/2024) as a second Override Drift precedent. The pattern now has RAIB documentation across multiple digests.

**Rule:** After coining a named framework from a RAIB report, search for other RAIB reports that document the same pattern. A follow-up post citing two or more instances is a stronger argument than the original post and positions the framework as a documented recurring failure mode, not a one-off observation.
