## Structural Analysis: What Went Wrong

### 1. Bullet Points in Post Body

The post used bullet points:
> "Each team saw part of the problem.
> - Structures saw the bridge.
> - Track saw the rail.
> - The warnings never combined..."

This is one of the documented failure patterns. Bullet points signal PowerPoint/report content to the algorithm. The post that preceded it (James/EMR) was written entirely in prose and achieved 16,623 impressions. The bullet structure here is the most likely single cause of underperformance.

**Rule confirmed:** Never use bullet points in LinkedIn posts. Write "Structures saw the bridge. Track saw the rail. The warnings never combined" as prose.

### 2. No Borrowed Credibility Opening

The opening is strong analytically but it is the author's framing, not an operator or practitioner voice. Compare:

**This post:** "In rail safety, two teams can both do their job, and the risk can still fall through the interface." ← Author's synthesis

**Shap post (37,726 impressions):** "The sensors detected movement 4 hours before the Shap landslip. But it was below the 10mm threshold for even a green alert." ← RAIB finding, specific and visceral

**Lesson:** Even for RAIB content, lead with the most specific, visceral fact from the report itself — not your interpretation of it. The RAIB finding ("no evidence of any joint visits") would have been a stronger opener than the analytical framing used.

### 3. Engagement Question Was Too Narrow

"Where do your critical warnings live? Are your teams seeing the whole picture, or just their half?"

This question is well-framed for rail safety professionals but too narrow for cross-industry reach. Compare to Shap's question: "How quickly do your monitoring investments become operationally useful?" — which any sector could answer.

The Audenshaw question implicitly requires knowledge of inspection databases and discipline structures. That filters the audience further than the content warrants.

## What the RAIB Formula Requires (Derived from Shap Success)

Based on the Shap post's 37,726 impressions vs Audenshaw's 1,446:

1. **Open with the most visceral fact from the RAIB report** (not your interpretation)
   - Good: "The sensors detected movement 4 hours before..."
   - Not: "In rail safety, two teams can both do their job..."

2. **Name two specific gaps with memorable labels**
   - Shap: "The Correlation Gap" + "The Deployment Gap"
   - Audenshaw: No named frameworks (missed opportunity — "The Discipline Gap" and "The Database Gap" were available)

3. **Write entirely in prose — zero bullet points**
   - Even lists of two or three items work better as "A did X. B did Y. C never happened."

4. **Make the engagement question cross-sector**
   - "How quickly do your monitoring investments become operationally useful?" (Shap) works for civil engineering, utilities, NHS, defence
   - "Where do your critical warnings live?" (Audenshaw) only works for rail safety

5. **Connect to the GBR transition explicitly** — Audenshaw did mention GBR but as an afterthought; Shap wove it through
